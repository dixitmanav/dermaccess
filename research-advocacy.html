Research & Advocacy

Disability access in dermatology remains under-discussed compared with other access barriers in medicine. This project highlights practical barriers that may occur before a patient ever reaches the exam room — including scheduling, interpreter access, portal accessibility, transportation, mobility accommodations, and clinic preparedness.

Current Work

An ongoing disability-access research project

I am currently involved in a disability-access research project examining how dermatology clinics across the United States respond to appointment requests from Deaf and blind patients. This work is separate from the educational resources on this website. The goal of this page is to translate broader themes from accessibility literature and public guidance into practical questions for dermatology care — not to present preliminary findings as established fact.

Important Caveat

Findings from ongoing research should not be considered final until the study is complete and reviewed through the appropriate scholarly process. The themes and questions described on this page reflect the framing of the research, not its conclusions.

Research Focus

Key research questions in disability-inclusive dermatology

These questions reflect gaps identified at the intersection of disability access scholarship, ADA guidance, and dermatology practice. They are framed to translate accessibility literature into actionable clinical inquiry.

1

Can Deaf patients reliably request interpreter access when scheduling dermatology appointments, and do clinics consistently fulfill these requests?

2

Are clinics prepared to communicate with blind patients about appointment logistics and visit instructions in accessible formats?

3

Do dermatology websites and patient portals provide information that is accessible to users with visual or cognitive disabilities?

4

How do clinic staff respond to disability accommodation requests at the time of scheduling, and where does the administrative process break down?

5

What practical, low-burden tools could improve accessibility at the pre-visit stage — before a patient even arrives at the clinic?

Why This Research Matters

Barriers before the exam room

Most discussions of healthcare access focus on what happens once a patient is in the room — the exam, the procedure, the communication. But for patients with disabilities, access often breaks down before the visit ever begins: during the phone call to schedule, in the intake form sent by portal, in the absence of interpreter arrangements, or in the assumption that the patient will simply figure it out.

Dermatology is a specialty that depends heavily on visual examination, verbal communication, and detailed instructions. These features make it both uniquely challenging for patients with certain disabilities and uniquely in need of disability-access research. The literature is growing, but much of the practical work remains to be done.

For Researchers & Advocates

Getting involved in dermatology disability access

If you are a researcher, student, advocate, or clinician interested in disability access in dermatology, the following areas represent meaningful opportunities to contribute.

Patient-Reported Barriers

Qualitative and survey-based research with Deaf, blind, mobility-disabled, and neurodivergent patients about their experiences in dermatology — what worked, what didn't, and what would help.

Clinic Readiness Assessment

Evaluating whether dermatology clinics have the infrastructure, trained staff, and documented processes to respond to disability accommodation requests at the scheduling stage.

Digital Accessibility Audits

Systematic assessment of dermatology clinic websites and patient portals for WCAG compliance, screen-reader compatibility, and accessible intake form design.

Curriculum Development

Building disability-inclusive dermatology training into medical student curricula, residency programs, and continuing medical education for practicing clinicians.

DermAccess

Practical, source-based resources to help make dermatology visits more accessible for patients with disabilities.
